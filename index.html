The Brief

From day-trippers to overnight guests

Lobster Land's gates have been busy — but most guests come, ride, eat, and leave. Management wants to know: could a hotel keep them around?

Borrowing pages from Disney and Universal's "stay and play" playbook, the park is weighing three lodging concepts: a modest family hotel, a premium coastal resort, or a tiered hybrid. Our team was hired as analytical consultants to figure out which guests would actually book, what package features they want, and how much it should cost.

This site is a tour of what we found.

Segmentation & Targeting

Who's already in the park?

A K-Means model on ten numeric guest variables (income, party size, distance traveled, experience preferences) surfaced four distinct personas. We chose k = 4 on business grounds — the silhouette and elbow plots didn't disagree, and four clusters give management a manageable, distinguishable set of guest types.

🏨

Current Overnight Guests

2.91 avg nights stayed

The only segment actually booking rooms today. Balanced taste — rides, shows, food. A "jack of all trades" guest already paying to stay.

Priority: High · Retention
🎭

Mature Show-Goers

$$$ highest daily spend

Older, small parties, culture-focused. They prefer shows over rides and spend big — but currently come and go in a single day.

Priority: Medium · Premium
🚗

Long-Haul Family Travelers

171 mi average distance

Largest parties, longest drives, highest household incomes — but tight per-day budgets. Natural overnight candidates from a logistics standpoint.

Priority: Med-High · Volume
🍤

Young Frequent Foodies

★ highest stay intent

Youngest, most frequent visitors, food-obsessed. They say they'd stay overnight — but rarely do. The biggest gap between intent and behavior.

Priority: High · Conversion
Where to focus: Current Overnight Guests defend existing revenue. Young Frequent Foodies are the conversion play — they already want to stay, they just need a reason that fits their wallet (and their Instagram feed).
Conjoint Analysis

Designing the package

We ran a ratings-based conjoint on stay_options.csv, joined the part-worth utilities to vendor costs, and brute-forced every possible bundle. Goal: maximize utility under the $150 per-guest cost cap finance handed us.

Classification

Who actually buys premium?

We trained Logistic Regression and Random Forest on stay_play_guests.csv to predict premium-package purchase. The Random Forest hit 94% on training data but cratered to 67% on test — textbook overfitting. Logistic Regression won.

Selected

Logistic Regression

71.8%Test Accuracy
68.7%Train Accuracy
+16.4 ppvs. naive baseline

Generalizes cleanly — test accuracy slightly above train, no overfitting, interpretable coefficients.

Random Forest

94.2%Train Accuracy
−27.5 pptrain-test gap

Memorized the training set. Even after GridSearchCV tuning, the gap stayed too wide to trust.

Top predictors of premium purchase

  • Stay intent score — buyers averaged 5.57 vs. 4.44 for non-buyers
  • Income — buyers earned ~$89k vs. ~$77k
  • Average daily spend — wallet behavior in-park predicts wallet behavior at the front desk
  • Distance traveled & nights already stayed
  • Segment 1 ("Ride Enthusiasts") behaved differently enough to keep as a feature
